# Deload and rest

> [!warning] Evidence: limited
> Rest days and lighter weeks are sound practice, but the scheduled in-training deload has thin trial support, unlike the well-evidenced race taper. Drive it by monitoring, not the calendar.

Rest days and periodic lighter weeks follow directly from the principle that adaptation happens during [recovery](supercompensation-and-adaptation.md). The principle is sound. The specific practice of a scheduled in-training deload week, however, has thinner evidence than its near-universal use suggests.

A supervised resistance-training trial found a one-week deload gave no advantage for size or power over training continuously, and endurance-specific deload trials are scarcer still ([Coleman et al. 2024](../sources/coleman-2024-deload.md)). The rationale rests largely on [periodisation](../techniques/periodisation.md) theory rather than direct evidence. This does not mean deloads are useless: they are a sensible hedge against accumulating fatigue and injury, and a reasonable default given how hard [overtraining](overtraining.md) is to detect early. It means the precise schedule, the common "every fourth week" rule, is convention, not a finding, and is better driven by [monitoring](training-monitoring.md) than by the calendar.

This contrasts sharply with the race [taper](../techniques/tapering.md), which is one of the best-quantified practices in the sport, with a 2 to 3% performance gain and well-defined parameters. The taper is evidence; the routine mid-block deload is reasonable practice.
