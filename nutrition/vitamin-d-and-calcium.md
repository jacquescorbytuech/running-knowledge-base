# Vitamin D and calcium

> [!info] Evidence: moderate
> Vitamin D deficiency is common in winter, at high latitudes and in indoor athletes, and both nutrients matter for bone. Correct a measured deficiency and meet calcium needs; do not megadose vitamin D on spec.

> [!warning] Not medical advice
> This is a general knowledge base, not medical or dietary advice. If you are injured, unwell or weighing up a supplement or a change to your diet, speak to a doctor, physiotherapist or registered dietitian who knows your situation.

Vitamin D and calcium earn a place because of bone, not performance. Runners load their skeletons hard, and a [bone stress injury](../injury/bone-stress-injuries.md) costs months. Keeping both nutrients adequate is cheap insurance for bone, but the case is for correcting deficiency, not for routine high-dose supplementation.

## Deficiency is common, and seasonal

Vitamin D is made in the skin from sunlight, so status falls in winter and at higher latitudes. A meta-analysis of athletes found a high prevalence of vitamin D inadequacy, with the greatest risk in winter, at higher latitudes and in indoor sports ([Farrokhyar et al. 2015](../sources/farrokhyar-2015-vitamin-d-athletes-meta.md)). A runner who trains outdoors in summer at a southern latitude is at lower risk than one who trains indoors through a northern winter, but neither can assume their status without a blood test.

## What the nutrients do

Vitamin D and calcium are both central to bone. Vitamin D supports calcium absorption and bone turnover, and persistently low status is associated with greater susceptibility to bone stress injury ([Owens et al. 2018](../sources/owens-2018-vitamin-d-athlete-review.md)). The role of vitamin D in muscle function and performance is often claimed but much less certain, and should not be the reason to supplement ([Owens et al. 2018](../sources/owens-2018-vitamin-d-athlete-review.md)).

## The supplementation case

> [!tip] Correct a measured deficiency, do not chase a high number
> Test, then treat to sufficiency. Recent reviews argue against megadosing: very high bolus doses drive vitamin D catabolism and may be counterproductive rather than helpful ([Owens et al. 2018](../sources/owens-2018-vitamin-d-athlete-review.md)). The aim is to move a deficient runner into the sufficient range, not to push status as high as possible.

The supporting trial evidence for bone comes mainly from combined supplementation. In a randomised trial of 5,201 female Navy recruits, daily 2,000 mg calcium plus 800 IU vitamin D during basic training cut stress-fracture incidence by about 20% ([Lappe et al. 2008](../sources/lappe-2008-calcium-vitamin-d-rct.md)). That population was under heavy new loading rather than trained running, so the size of the effect may not carry across directly. It is also worth noting that a large meta-analysis of 81 RCTs (53,537 participants) found vitamin D supplementation alone had no effect on fractures, falls, or bone mineral density, leading its authors to conclude there is little justification for using vitamin D to protect musculoskeletal health in the general population ([Bolland et al. 2018](../sources/bolland-2018-vitamin-d-musculoskeletal.md)). The case for supplementation therefore rests on correcting a measured deficiency and meeting calcium needs together, not on high-dose vitamin D in isolation.

## Calcium for bone

Calcium is the building block of bone, and intake tracks injury risk. A review of young athletes found that higher calcium and dairy intake was associated with lower stress-fracture rates, with the largest reductions in female athletes taking in more than 1,500 mg per day ([Tenforde et al. 2010](../sources/tenforde-2010-calcium-vitamin-d-stress-fracture.md)). Food sources, dairy in particular, are the first port of call; a runner who avoids dairy needs to find calcium elsewhere and may need to supplement to hit target.

> [!warning] Calcium and energy availability are not interchangeable
> Calcium and vitamin D help, but they cannot rescue bone in a runner who is chronically underfuelling. Low energy availability suppresses the hormones that build bone, and that has to be fixed at source. See [RED-S](red-s.md) and [bone stress injuries](../injury/bone-stress-injuries.md).
