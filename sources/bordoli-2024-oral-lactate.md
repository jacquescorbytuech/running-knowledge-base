# Bordoli et al. 2024, Oral lactate supplementation, acid-base balance and prolonged interval cycling

A randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover trial in 16 trained male cyclists (VO₂max 59 ± 7 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹). Participants took 120 mg·kg⁻¹ body mass of calcium lactate or placebo 70 minutes before a roughly two-hour protocol of five repeated blocks (moderate cycling, a 1 km time trial, higher-intensity cycling, a 4 km time trial). Lactate raised blood bicarbonate and the strong ion difference and lowered rating of perceived exertion, but did not improve time-trial performance, and it significantly increased gastrointestinal symptoms.

## Key takeaways

- Oral lactate (calcium lactate, 120 mg·kg⁻¹, 70 min pre-exercise) produced no performance benefit over placebo in trained cyclists (overall completion time p = 0.320).
- It did shift acid-base balance in the expected direction: higher bicarbonate (p = 0.041) and strong ion difference (p = 0.026), consistent with a buffering rather than a fuelling effect.
- Perceived exertion fell (p = 0.012) despite unchanged performance.
- Gastrointestinal side effects (flatulence, cramps, belching, stomach ache, bowel urgency) rose significantly with lactate.
- The authors note the dose may have been insufficient and call for work on alternative lactate forms and dosing, i.e. the ergogenic case is unproven, not settled.
