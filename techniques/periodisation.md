# Periodisation

> [!warning] Evidence: limited
> The general base-to-specific logic is sound. Claims that one specific scheme reliably beats another are weakly evidenced.

Periodisation is the organisation of training into phases over weeks and months, typically progressing from a general [base](base-building.md) through more specific work to a [taper](tapering.md) before competition. The broad logic, progressive overload and a base-to-specific sequence, is uncontroversial and supported in principle.

The weakly evidenced part is the belief that a particular *scheme* is superior to another. Block periodisation, which concentrates training stress into sequential focused blocks, is proposed as better than traditional concurrent development for trained athletes ([Issurin 2016](../sources/issurin-2016-periodisation.md)), but the head-to-head literature is small, heterogeneous and at high risk of bias, with no robust general superiority ([Mølmen et al. 2019](../sources/molmen-2019-block.md)).

Kiely's critiques are the sharpest, and they are well argued. The major periodisation models share a mid-twentieth-century heritage whose founding assumptions, including a literal interpretation of the general adaptation syndrome, are no longer scientifically justifiable; head-to-head comparisons of schemes tend to give equivalent group outcomes while individual responses vary widely ([Kiely 2012](../sources/kiely-2012-periodisation.md); [Kiely 2018](../sources/kiely-2018-periodisation.md)). His conclusion is not to abandon planning but to make it responsive and individualised rather than formulaic.

The honest summary: structure training, progress it, and taper for races, because that general approach is sound. Treat any claim that scheme X reliably beats scheme Y as poorly supported, and tune the plan to the [individual](../concepts/individual-variation.md) rather than to a named template. This is closely related to the [supercompensation and adaptation](../recovery/supercompensation-and-adaptation.md) model, whose textbook curve is itself an oversimplification.

## A sensible default

> [!tip] What to do given the weak evidence
> Since no scheme is proven superior, the defensible approach is the simple one almost every system shares: build a [base](base-building.md) of mostly easy volume, then layer on progressively more race-specific [intensity](interval-training.md), then [taper](tapering.md) for the race. Make it responsive rather than rigid, adjusting from how training is actually being absorbed ([monitoring](../recovery/training-monitoring.md)) rather than forcing a fixed template. The general progression helps; the elaborate scheme on top of it is where the evidence runs out.
