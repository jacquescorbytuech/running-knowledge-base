# Treadmill running

> [!info] Evidence: moderate
> A treadmill is a valid substitute for outdoor running, with broadly similar mechanics and oxygen cost. The famous "set it to 1%" rule is a rough, speed-dependent correction for the missing air resistance, not a universal law: it matters at tempo pace and faster and is needless for easy running. The main real downsides are heat build-up and race-specificity.

Treadmill and overground running are biomechanically much alike, with similar stride timing, length and peak vertical loading and only minor differences in footstrike kinematics and propulsion ([Van Hooren et al. 2020](../sources/van-hooren-2020-treadmill.md)). Submaximal oxygen cost is broadly preserved — VO₂ is similar to overground at matched speeds — though blood lactate runs lower and heart rate and perceived effort show speed-dependent differences, elevated at faster speeds and reduced at slower ones ([Miller et al. 2019](../sources/miller-2019-treadmill-overground.md)). Endurance performance is meaningfully poorer on a motorised treadmill than overground, even when the physiological measures look alike, so use it for fitness building but not as a race-effort substitute ([Miller et al. 2019](../sources/miller-2019-treadmill-overground.md)). As a way to train fitness, the treadmill is a genuine stand-in.

## The 1% rule, honestly

Indoors there is no air to push through, so at the same speed a treadmill is slightly easier than running outdoors. The well-known fix, a 1% gradient, comes from a single study of nine trained runners and made treadmill cost match outdoor cost across roughly 2.92 to 5.0 m/s (about 3:48 to 5:43 per km); at slower paces no incline was needed ([Jones & Doust 1996](../sources/jones-doust-1996-treadmill-grade.md)). The rule is therefore speed-dependent, not absolute. Air resistance is a small share of the energy cost at distance pace — around 8% at 5 km pace — but grows steeply with speed because the cost scales with its cube, reaching roughly 16% at sprint pace ([Pugh 1970](../sources/pugh-1970-air-resistance.md)), which means the gradient correction needed to match outdoor cost is likewise speed-dependent, increasing from negligible at easy pace to well above 1% at sprint speed. Treat 1% as a sensible default for tempo-and-faster sessions and ignore it for easy runs.

## Uses and limits

The treadmill earns its place for control: precise pacing, programmed [incline work](hill-training.md), safety in the dark or on ice, and steady conditions for a key session. Two limits are worth knowing. First, standing still relative to the air means far less convective cooling, so heat builds faster than outdoors at the same effort; a fan and ventilation matter, and the heat link is the [thermoregulation](../concepts/thermoregulation.md) one. Second, the setting can shift submaximal economy: high-level runners were measurably more economical on a track than on a treadmill at the same speed, even though their VO₂max was unchanged ([Mooses et al. 2015](../sources/mooses-2015-field-economy.md)), and the belt and fixed environment make it less specific to an outdoor race. Individual gait is a further caveat: a wearable study found a single lab or treadmill session represented a given runner's real-world gait only loosely, so treadmill-based form analysis of one runner should be read with caution ([Davis et al. 2024](../sources/davis-2024-inlab-vs-realworld-gait.md)). Use it for what it does well, and keep enough outdoor running for [race specificity](race-pacing.md).
